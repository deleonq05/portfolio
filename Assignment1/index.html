Design and AI

Quinn de Leon, 2025

There are things that just go well with each other. Peanut butter and jelly, your bed and your pillow, and many more. But design and AI is something that weighs on a lot of people's minds. And for a good reason, there are a lot of art that are being currently replicated by AI and User Experience is not an exception to their iteration. In Design is Dead by Michael F. Buckley, the topic of AI being side by side with design is explored. The idea that User Experience and design skills are becoming obsolete is prominent in Buckley's article. But the main idea is that your artwork is not losing to AI, but someone who knows AI better than you. But what is User Experience? User Experience Design, or UX Design, encompasses all elements of something as perceived by users, including a website, landing page, their own identity, and the service itself. There is UX design everywhere. It is integrated into everything you use, including websites and apps, automobile console controls, and even the grip on kitchen appliances like can openers. There are also more specific uses for UX design, such as in medical gadgets and surgical instruments. To put it briefly, when UX design is done right, it can help launch better products with a higher chance of success. Businesses that incorporate a well-considered UX design strategy into their product development processes not only improve the end user's experience but also increase profits and reduce liability.

On the other hand, The term artificial intelligence (AI) refers to a group of technologies that enable computers to carry out operations that normally call for human intelligence. AI systems are capable of learning, problem-solving, and decision-making. There are pros and cons in using artificial intelligence. Artificial intelligence (AI) has been a game-changer in many industries, offering a ton of benefits, but also raising some concerns. One of the major pros is efficiency. AI can handle repetitive tasks and process large amounts of data much faster than us. This has been helpful in fields like healthcare, where AI helps in customer service, where chatbots can assist customers at any time of day. AI also allows for more personalization in things like shopping and content recommendations, making our lives more convenient. On the flip side, one of the biggest downsides is the fear of job displacement. As AI becomes more capable, there's a real concern that it could replace jobs in sectors like manufacturing, transportation, and most especially in creative fields. Plus, there's the issue of privacy. AI systems often rely on massive amounts of data, which can raise questions about how our personal information is being used or misused. There's also the worry of AI making decisions that we don't fully understand or can't control, like in the case of autonomous vehicles or algorithms that affect hiring practices.

As a UX designer, I have a lot of mixed feelings about AI creeping into the design space. Sure, it's a good tool to speed up certain tasks, like automating user testing or analyzing data to find trends, but I'm not fully sold on the idea of AI being the end result of a design project. The creative side of design involves empathy, understanding nuanced human behavior, and making decisions based on subtle feedback from real users—things that, honestly, I don't think AI can fully replicate. It can generate layouts or suggest color schemes based on algorithms, but that doesn't mean it's capturing the essence of what a user truly feels when interacting with a product. I mean, sure, AI can be a helpful assistant, but can it truly replace the instinct and understanding that comes from working closely with real people? AI should remain a tool—like an extra set of hands for data crunching or repetitive tasks—rather than taking over the actual design process. If we let AI dictate the outcome, it might create designs that look nice on paper but miss the mark on connecting with users on a deeper level. At the end of the day, design is about humans designing for humans, and I'm not convinced that AI can replace that heart of the process.
